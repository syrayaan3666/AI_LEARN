Abstract
The vigorous development of artificial intelligence (AI) represented
by large language models (LLMs) has rapidly promoted the updat-
ing and development of educational technology. Agentic workflows
(A Ws) built based on LLMs can realize complex tasks in the field
of education, which allows the emergence of swarm intelligence
(SI) through multi-agent collaboration [1]. This study introduces
the Agent4EDU (agent for education) framework, which outlines 4
application models in education from the two dimensions of degree
of agency and degree of interaction, including human-AI collabo-
ration, AI assistant, instruction execution, and general type. The
proposed Agent4EDU framework discusses the paradigm of educa-
tional applications of AI agents and promotes the development of
the field of AI for education.

2 Paradigm Shift: Multi-agent systems drive AI
change in education
2.1 Emergence of Swarm Intelligence: the
Transition from Single-Agent to
Multi-Agent System
Swarm intelligence (SI) originates from the study of social animal
behavior. Individuals in these populations can generate powerful SI
to deal with real-world problems through simple interaction rules
of self-organization [2]. In MAS, multi-agent collaboration can also
promote the emergence of SI [ 3]. Here, we formally describe the
educational application of MAS using Equation 3), where n is the
number of agents in MAS. These agents are coupled through A W
and call LLMs to form and improve the final output. Multi-agent
collaboration can effectively deal with complex problems in educa-
tional scenarios, which exceeds the task processing capabilities of
single-agent. The SI that emerges in MAS enables it to better meet
the needs of learners.

Agent, as a concept in the field of computer science, is defined
as an entity that can perceive its environment and make decisions
on its own. It is characterized by autonomy and flexibility and has
been widely used in education. Specifically, in terms of learning
methods, Bruner [ 4] and Ramadhevi [ 5] used agents to promote
collaborative learning, which was achieved through agent-based
simulation. Agents are also used to form the core of distance ed-
ucation framework in order to provide personalized courses and
learning experiences[6]. In terms of training applications, agent-
based frameworks are also used to establish security training and
corporate education to promote knowledge transfer [ 7-8]. Finally,
in terms of educational support tools, Stummer[9], Walters [10] and

As technology develops, learners‚Äô educational needs are becom-

MAS has also begun to be widely used in the field of education
due to its stronger task processing capabilities in real scenarios.

2.2 Transition of Paradigm: From Preset Models
to LLM-Driven Evolution
LLM-based MAS is designed to perceive the environment and make
precise decisions based on perception to maximize its performance
in real-world tasks. By using LLM as the core of systems for edu-
cational purposes, these systems are able to perceive, reason, and
make decisions and actions autonomously. LLM provides direct
processing of natural language to efficiently solve educational tasks.
This promotes the change of paradigm from preset models to LLM-
driven evolution. For example, online writing platforms may have
only been able to make revision suggestions based on semantics
and polishing rules, but now they can use LLM-based MAS to gen-
erate more specific revision suggestions, thereby effectively helping
learners improve their writing skills.
At present, the research and development of agent-based edu-
cation systems mainly focuses on the education platforms devel-
opment and learning analytics. In terms of education platform
development , Khasianov [ 18] developed a multi-agent education
platform that uses agents to enhance learners‚Äô initiative and thus
speed up their knowledge acquisition efficiency. In order to further
improve the quality of teaching, Yan [19] developed an agent-based
computer network-assisted teaching platform. In terms of learning
analytics, Chen [ 20] and Simon [ 21] introduced agents to analyze
public health and higher education systems, thereby providing
traceable data support for policy making and long-term develop-
ment. Hantono [ 22] and Bruner [ 23] discussed the potential of
augmented reality and agent networks in improving learning effi-
ciency and motivation.
LLM can process complex natural language input and dynam-
ically adjust responses based on context and user input, which
makes LLM-based educational systems more practical. By inte-
grating LLM-based agents in educational systems, those systems‚Äô
language processing and flexible interaction capabilities are greatly
enhanced. For example, Zheng et al. [ 24] used an LLM-supported
embedded agent for language understanding and sub-goal plan-
ning, and constructed semantic graphs through visual observation
to obtain symbolic representations. MathBoard proposed by Jiang
et al. [ 25] is based on LLM and gradually generates board writing
(BW) by integrating a multi-agent collaborative framework, thereby
effectively promoting learners‚Äô knowledge construction.
2.3 Knowledge Customization: Transitioning
from General Domains to Subject-Specific
Teaching
At present, agent-based systems have been widely used in educa-
tion, medicine, finance and other fields. In the field of information
retrieval, Coneglian [ 26] used agents to effectively extract infor-
mation to promote the efficiency and accuracy of information re-
trieval. In the field of control engineering, Ma [ 27]‚Äôs research on
multi-agent collaboration integrated the efficient task processing
capabilities of agents into the control engineering classroom to im-
prove the educational model. In the field of education, researchers
such as Yang [ 28], Yonah [29], Alexandru [ 30], and Jawahar [ 31]
used AI agents as core components of teaching platforms, providing
learners with real-time feedback and personalized support to opti-
mize the learning process and promote interaction. Furthermore,
Gu [32]‚Äôs work on higher education simulation further verified the
effectiveness of agents in educational system analysis.
Focusing on the application of agents in the field of education,
they can provide technical support for teaching scenarios such
as personalized learning, automatic question and answer, and So-
cratic guidance. Their application scope has gradually shifted from
general knowledge fields to more targeted subject teaching. For
example, Qi [33] proposed the IntelliChain framework, an educa-
tional application framework for enhanced Socratic dialogue with
LLMs and knowledge graphs. Xie [ 34] and Yan [ 35] focused on
physical education, using a multi-agent teaching management sys-
tem and a computer network-assisted teaching platform to promote
the all-round development of students by improving teaching ef-
ficiency. Samra [ 36] applied data mining agents and intelligent
simulation technology to clinical skills education, improving clini-
cal teaching effectiveness by optimizing the learning environment.
Dubovi [ 37] used an interpretable agent-based model (ABM) to
reveal the specific advantages of biological learning by making
micro-mechanisms visible. Although agent technology has brought
more flexible and personalized learning experiences to education
and effectively improved the efficiency of teaching and learning
[38], the ethical issues of these educational technologies must be
considered to ensure the credibility and sustainable development
of educational technologies [39-40].
3 Agent4EDU supported Educational
Application Framework
The application of AI agents in the field of education has produced
many specific cases, in which agents are often used to play roles
such as instructors and study partners to promote learners‚Äô knowl-
edge construction process and provide timely emotional support
to learners. To better analyze the application types of agents in
education, we divide the educational applications of Agent4EDU
into four levels according to the autonomy of agents and the depth
of human-AI interaction, as shown in Figure 2, where the inter-
action level is divided into general type, AI assistant, instruction
executor and human-AI collaboration type from shallow to deep;
the autonomy level is divided into general type, instruction ex-
ecutor, human-AI collaboration and AI assistant from low to high
[41]. Overall, the interaction depth and autonomy level of the

3.1 Human-AI Collaboration
Human-AI collaboration is defined as the highest level of interac-
tion in the Agent4EDU framework. In this type, the AI represented
by agents works closely with users, such as collaborating on a
painting or art creation. In this mode, agents do not just respond
to commands or provide basic learning support; instead, agents col-
laborate and co-create with users in depth. The main task of agents
is to identify learners‚Äô learning needs and goals, interact through
the user interface to obtain user input and feedback, and improve
output based on this information to advance the improvement of
educational tasks. The human-AI collaboration type is suitable for
educational scenarios such as virtual laboratories and collabora-
tive learning, which enhances the interactivity of learning, thereby
potentially increasing learners‚Äô enthusiasm and participation.
3.2 AI Assistant
The AI assistant type is at the highest level of autonomy, where
agents can proactively make decisions and provide effective learn-
ing support to learners. By analyzing learners‚Äô learning data and
behavior information, the AI assistant played by the agent can
quickly understand the user‚Äôs needs and feedback, and create per-
sonalized learning suggestions and learning resource suggestions
based on the user‚Äôs preferences. When the AI assistant receives in-
put or requests from learners, it can use its knowledge database and
algorithms to analyze the input, generate suggestions in combina-
tion with historical learning data, and wait for further feedback or
requests until the learner makes a decision. This interactive process
will continue until the problem is resolved. By providing precise
and effective support, agents can bring a personalized learning
experience to learners.
3.3 Instruction Execution
In the instruction execution mode, agents receive commands from
learners and perform tasks as instructed. This makes the system
functionality easy to implement. These agent-based educational
applications can meet different learner needs and make the learning
experience more personalized. The A W begins when the agent
receives an explicit command from the learner. The agent looks

such as searching for information or tutoring learners to complete
homework. This mode is well suited for automated learning tasks
such as vocabulary memorization and standardized testing, but it
is weakly coupled with learners at the collaborative level.
3.4 General Type
General-type agents form the foundational layer. These agents
provide basic educational resources and services, such as online
courses and learning databases, and are able to perform routine
tasks and simple requests. In this mode, agents can handle simple
queries and interactions, and usually involve one-way information
transfer. These agents have low autonomy. When learners‚Äô requests
are sent to agents, agents will call predefined functions or services to
respond. The solutions it provides do not have complex processing
and decision-making processes. This type of agent is suitable for
providing basic learning resources, answering common questions,
or providing course and grade information.
4 Conclusion
In this research, we review the efforts and achievements of agents
in applying AI to education. It also introduces the Agent4EDU
framework. By exploring the integration of large language models
and agent technology, A Ws provide new solutions for personalized
and adaptive learning, improve students‚Äô learning efficiency and
experience, reduce teachers‚Äô workload, and promote continuous
innovation in educational technology.
